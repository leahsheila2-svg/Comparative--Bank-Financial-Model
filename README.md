# Model Architecture & Analytical Workflow

The Comparative Bank Financial Model has been developed using a modular financial modelling architecture where each worksheet performs a distinct analytical function while remaining dynamically linked to every subsequent stage of the workbook.

Rather than acting as independent schedules, every worksheet contributes to a structured analytical workflow that transforms historical accounting information into investment intelligence.

Each worksheet progressively answers a different investment question.

| Worksheet | Primary Question Answered |
|------------|---------------------------|
| Financial Comparison | How have both institutions historically performed? |
| Common-Size Analysis | How do the financial structures compare irrespective of size? |
| Horizontal Trend Analysis | Is financial performance improving or deteriorating over time? |
| Financial Ratio Analysis | Which institution is fundamentally stronger? |
| Banking Performance Benchmarking | Which bank outperforms its peer across key banking metrics? |
| Comparative Peer Analysis | Where does each institution possess competitive advantages? |
| DuPont Analysis | What drives shareholder returns? |
| Relative Valuation | Are either of the institutions mispriced by the market? |
| Sensitivity Analysis | How robust are valuation estimates under changing assumptions? |
| Investment Summary | Which institution offers the superior investment opportunity? |

Rather than relying upon a single profitability ratio or valuation multiple, the model integrates multiple analytical techniques into a unified investment framework. This layered methodology allows users to understand not only **what** has happened historically, but **why** performance differs between the institutions and **how** those differences influence shareholder value.

# Financial Comparison

## Overview

The Financial Comparison worksheet serves as the foundation of the entire financial model. Every subsequent ratio, benchmark, valuation multiple and investment conclusion originates from the historical financial information analysed within this section.

Financial statements provide a historical record of management performance. However, reviewing standalone financial statements rarely provides sufficient insight into operational quality or investment attractiveness. The Financial Comparison worksheet addresses this limitation by standardising historical financial data for both institutions into a consistent analytical framework.

This enables direct comparison across all major financial statement components and establishes the historical context required for more advanced analytical techniques.

Rather than asking **"Which bank generated higher profits?"**, the worksheet seeks to answer a broader set of questions:

- Which institution generated stronger revenue growth?
- Which bank expanded its earnings more efficiently?
- Which institution controlled operating costs more effectively?
- Which bank strengthened its balance sheet?
- Which institution demonstrated greater financial resilience?
- Which bank created more sustainable shareholder value?

The outputs from this worksheet form the basis of every subsequent analysis contained within the model.

# Interest Income

## Purpose

Interest Income measures the total revenue earned from interest-bearing assets including customer loans, government securities, treasury investments and other interest-generating financial assets.

For commercial banks, Interest Income represents the primary source of operating revenue and provides an indication of the institution's lending capacity, market penetration and balance sheet size.

Institutions capable of consistently expanding Interest Income generally demonstrate:

- Strong lending activity.
- Growing customer demand.
- Effective asset deployment.
- Expanding interest-earning assets.
- Sustainable business growth.

However, higher Interest Income does not necessarily imply greater profitability. Revenue must always be analysed alongside funding costs, operating expenses and credit impairment.

---

## Formula

Interest Income = Income from Loans + Income from Investment Securities + Other Interest-Earning Assets

---

## Model Findings

| Institution | FY2025 Interest Income |
|-------------|----------------------:|
| **KCB Group PLC** | **KES 275.4 Billion** |
| **NCBA Group PLC** | **KES 81.8 Billion** |

KCB generated significantly higher Interest Income, reflecting its larger loan portfolio, diversified regional operations and broader customer base.

NCBA reported lower absolute Interest Income due to its comparatively smaller balance sheet. However, absolute revenue should not be interpreted in isolation, as larger institutions naturally generate higher income by virtue of their scale.

---

## Investor Interpretation

Interest Income primarily measures business scale rather than business quality.

While KCB's larger revenue base demonstrates market leadership and earning capacity, investors should evaluate whether this higher revenue is converted efficiently into shareholder returns.

Consequently, the model proceeds to analyse Net Interest Income, profitability margins and operating efficiency before drawing investment conclusions.

# Net Interest Income (NII)

## Purpose

Net Interest Income represents the difference between interest earned on productive assets and interest paid on deposits and other funding sources.

It measures the profitability generated from the bank's core intermediation function—borrowing funds at one rate and lending them at a higher rate.

For commercial banks, Net Interest Income is widely regarded as one of the most important indicators of operating performance because it reflects the success of the institution's primary business model.

---

## Formula

Net Interest Income = Interest Income − Interest Expense

---

## Why It Matters

A bank may generate substantial Interest Income, but if funding costs increase proportionately, profitability will remain unchanged.

Net Interest Income therefore measures the institution's ability to generate earnings after accounting for the cost of obtaining funds.

Growth in Net Interest Income may result from:

- Higher lending volumes.
- Improved Net Interest Margins.
- Better funding mix.
- Lower funding costs.
- Improved asset pricing.

---

## Model Findings

| Institution | FY2025 Net Interest Income | YoY Growth |
|-------------|--------------------------:|-----------:|
| **KCB Group PLC** | **KES 174.8 Billion** | **11.2%** |
| **NCBA Group PLC** | **KES 57.7 Billion** | **21.6%** |

Although KCB generated significantly higher absolute Net Interest Income, NCBA recorded almost twice the rate of annual growth.

This indicates that NCBA improved the profitability of its lending activities at a faster pace during the reporting period.

---

## What This Says About the Business

KCB's larger Net Interest Income reflects the scale of its banking franchise and its ability to generate substantial recurring earnings from lending operations.

NCBA's stronger growth rate suggests improving pricing power, enhanced funding efficiency and stronger momentum in its core banking business.

For long-term investors, sustained growth in Net Interest Income often indicates strengthening competitive positioning and improved earnings sustainability.

---

## Investment Interpretation

The analysis highlights a distinction between **scale** and **growth**.

KCB demonstrates superior earning capacity due to its larger balance sheet.

NCBA demonstrates stronger momentum through faster expansion of core banking profitability.

Institutional investors frequently seek businesses capable of combining both characteristics—large earning capacity with sustainable growth.

The remaining sections of the model determine whether these differences ultimately translate into superior profitability, stronger shareholder returns and greater intrinsic value.


# Operating Income

## Purpose

Operating Income measures the total income generated from both interest-generating and non-interest-generating activities before deducting operating expenses and taxation.

Unlike Interest Income, which focuses solely on lending activities, Operating Income provides a broader assessment of the bank's revenue-generating capability by incorporating:

- Net Interest Income
- Fees and Commission Income
- Foreign Exchange Trading Income
- Investment Income
- Trading Securities Income
- Other Operating Revenue

For commercial banks, diversified operating income is generally considered an indicator of business resilience because institutions are less dependent upon a single source of revenue.

Banks capable of generating substantial non-funded income are often better positioned to withstand fluctuations in lending activity and changing interest rate environments.

---

## Formula

Operating Income

=

Net Interest Income

+

Non-Interest Income

---

## Why It Matters

Operating Income demonstrates the ability of management to generate recurring earnings from multiple business segments.

Consistent growth in Operating Income generally reflects:

- Strong customer acquisition.
- Effective product diversification.
- Improved transaction volumes.
- Successful treasury operations.
- Expansion into fee-based banking services.
- Higher quality recurring revenue.

Because commercial banks operate across multiple revenue streams, investors frequently examine Operating Income rather than Interest Income alone when evaluating long-term earnings sustainability.

---

## Model Findings

| Institution | FY2025 Operating Income |
|-------------|-----------------------:|
| **KCB Group PLC** | **KES 227.0 Billion** |
| **NCBA Group PLC** | **KES 71.8 Billion** |

KCB generated significantly higher Operating Income owing to its larger regional footprint, broader customer base and diversified banking operations.

NCBA generated a comparatively smaller revenue base but recorded stronger year-on-year growth, indicating continued expansion of its core banking franchise.

---

## What This Says About the Business

KCB continues to benefit from economies of scale and diversified income generation across multiple East African markets.

Its ability to consistently generate substantial Operating Income demonstrates the strength of its franchise, market penetration and product diversification.

NCBA, despite operating at a smaller scale, continues to improve the quality and sustainability of its revenue base through stronger operating momentum.

This distinction becomes increasingly important when profitability ratios are evaluated later within the model.

---

## Investor Interpretation

Large operating income alone does not necessarily create shareholder value.

The key question is:

**How much of that operating income ultimately becomes profit?**

To answer this question, investors compare Operating Income with:

- Operating Expenses
- Credit Impairment Charges
- Profit Before Tax
- Return on Assets
- Return on Equity

Only then can earnings quality be properly assessed.

# Operating Expenses

## Purpose

Operating Expenses represent the costs incurred in generating banking income and maintaining day-to-day operations.

The worksheet evaluates the institution's ability to control expenditure while continuing to expand revenue-generating activities.

Typical operating expenses include:

- Employee Costs
- Administrative Expenses
- Occupancy Costs
- Information Technology
- Marketing
- Regulatory Expenses
- Professional Fees
- Depreciation and Amortisation

Unlike manufacturing businesses, commercial banks rely heavily upon human capital and technology infrastructure.

Consequently, expense management is one of the most important determinants of profitability.

---

## Formula

Operating Expenses

=

Personnel Costs

+

Administrative Costs

+

Other Operating Costs

---

## Why It Matters

Revenue growth alone does not create shareholder value.

Institutions must also demonstrate operational discipline.

When operating expenses increase more slowly than revenue, profitability improves through **positive operating leverage**.

Conversely, expense growth exceeding revenue growth gradually erodes earnings quality.

Operating expense analysis therefore measures management's ability to convert revenue growth into sustainable profitability.

---

- Expanded technology infrastructure.

NCBA maintained a considerably leaner cost structure despite continuing to grow operating income.

---

## What This Says About the Business

Higher operating costs should never be interpreted negatively without considering the scale of the institution.

KCB's higher expense base reflects the complexity and scale of managing one of East Africa's largest banking groups.

NCBA demonstrates stronger cost discipline, suggesting management has been successful in controlling expenditure while continuing to expand operations.

---

## Investor Interpretation

Investors rarely evaluate expenses independently.

Instead they ask:

**Is revenue growing faster than costs?**

If Operating Income expands more rapidly than Operating Expenses, the institution benefits from increasing operating leverage.

Positive operating leverage ultimately translates into:

- Higher operating margins.
- Stronger profitability.
- Improved earnings quality.
- Greater shareholder returns.

# Credit Impairment Charges

## Purpose

Credit Impairment Charges represent provisions recognised for expected credit losses arising from deterioration in the quality of customer loans and advances.

Banks are exposed to credit risk because lending represents their principal business activity.

Consequently, expected credit losses represent one of the largest uncertainties affecting banking profitability.

The impairment analysis evaluates the quality of the loan portfolio together with management's approach toward credit risk.

---

## Formula

Credit Impairment

=

Expected Credit Losses recognised under IFRS 9

---

## Why It Matters

Credit impairment directly reduces profitability.

However, higher impairment charges do not necessarily indicate poor management.

They may also reflect:

- Conservative provisioning.
- Larger loan books.
- Temporary economic weakness.
- Increased regulatory requirements.

Investors therefore evaluate impairment alongside:

- Loan Growth
- Non-Performing Loans
- Capital Adequacy
- Return on Assets

rather than independently.

---

## Model Findings

| **NCBA Group PLC** | **KES 6.9 Billion** |

NCBA reported substantially lower impairment expenses, contributing positively to earnings growth and profitability.

---

## What This Says About the Business

KCB's higher impairment expense illustrates the trade-off associated with scale.

Larger loan books generate higher interest income but also expose the institution to greater credit risk.

NCBA's comparatively lower impairment burden suggests either:

- Better asset quality.
- More conservative lending.
- Lower exposure to higher-risk borrowers.
- More favourable economic conditions within its lending portfolio.

---

## Investor Interpretation

Investors generally prefer institutions capable of maintaining:

- Stable loan growth.

The interaction between these variables provides insight into the quality of management's credit underwriting processes.

For this reason, impairment analysis serves as an important bridge between revenue generation and shareholder value creation.

# Profit Before Tax (PBT)

## Purpose

Profit Before Tax measures earnings generated after operating expenses and impairment charges but before taxation.

It provides one of the clearest measures of operating profitability because it removes differences created by varying tax rates and focuses purely on business performance.

---

## Formula

Profit Before Tax

=

Operating Income

−

Operating Expenses

−

Credit Impairment

# Common-Size Financial Statement Analysis

## Overview

Historical financial statements present financial information in absolute monetary values. While these figures provide insight into the scale of an institution, they are often insufficient when comparing companies of different sizes.

For example, KCB Group PLC generates substantially higher operating income than NCBA Group PLC. However, this does not necessarily imply superior financial performance. Larger institutions naturally report higher revenues, assets and profits because they operate with larger balance sheets and broader customer bases.

To overcome this limitation, the model converts the financial statements into **common-size percentages**, allowing every line item to be expressed relative to a meaningful financial base.

This process removes the influence of company size and enables direct comparison of financial structure, profitability and cost allocation between institutions.

Rather than asking:

> "Which bank generated more revenue?"

Common-size analysis asks:

> "How efficiently does each bank generate and allocate its revenue?"

This distinction transforms financial statement analysis from a comparison of size into a comparison of quality.

---

# Purpose of Common-Size Analysis

The objective of common-size analysis is to standardise financial statements by expressing each account as a percentage of a base figure.

This enables analysts to:

- Compare banks of different sizes on an equal basis.
- Evaluate cost structures.
- Assess profitability margins.
- Identify changes in financial composition over time.
- Benchmark operating performance against competitors.
- Detect structural strengths and weaknesses.
- Improve interpretation of financial ratios.

By eliminating differences caused by scale, the analysis provides a clearer view of operational efficiency and financial quality.

---

# Common-Size Income Statement

## Purpose

The Common-Size Income Statement expresses every income statement line item as a percentage of Operating Income.

This illustrates how every shilling of revenue is distributed across funding costs, operating expenses, impairment charges and net profit.

The analysis answers questions such as:

- How much revenue is consumed by operating costs?
- How much revenue is lost to impairment?
- How much revenue ultimately becomes profit?
- Which institution converts revenue into shareholder earnings more efficiently?

---

## Formula

Common-Size Percentage

=

(Financial Statement Line Item ÷ Operating Income)

×100

---

## Why It Matters

Two banks may generate identical revenue growth yet produce significantly different profits.

The difference often lies in their cost structure.

Common-size analysis enables investors to determine:

- Which institution operates more efficiently.
- Which bank controls costs more effectively.
- Which business generates superior operating margins.
- Which institution possesses stronger earnings quality.

This analysis therefore bridges historical accounting information and profitability analysis.

---

## Model Interpretation

The common-size income statement within the model demonstrates that:

- KCB allocates a larger proportion of revenue towards operating expenses and impairment provisions.
- NCBA converts a greater proportion of operating income into bottom-line earnings.

This finding supports the observation made within the Financial Comparison worksheet that NCBA operates with a leaner and more efficient operating structure.

Although KCB generates substantially greater revenue in absolute terms, NCBA retains a higher proportion of every shilling earned.

---

## Investor Interpretation

For investors, common-size analysis provides insight into the sustainability of profitability.

Institutions capable of consistently retaining a larger proportion of operating income generally exhibit:

- Better cost discipline.
- Higher earnings quality.
- Greater pricing power.
- Improved operational efficiency.
- Stronger long-term profitability.

This suggests that profitability is influenced not only by revenue generation but also by effective cost management.

# Horizontal Trend Analysis

## Overview

While common-size analysis compares financial structure, Horizontal Trend Analysis evaluates how financial performance evolves over time.

Rather than examining a single reporting period, this worksheet measures the direction and magnitude of historical financial change.

Trend analysis enables investors to determine whether financial performance is improving, deteriorating or remaining stable.

This is particularly important because successful investments are often driven by improving fundamentals rather than historical size alone.

---

# Purpose

The objective of Horizontal Trend Analysis is to measure the rate of change in financial performance across reporting periods.

The worksheet evaluates historical trends in:

- Revenue.

- Profit After Tax.
- Total Assets.
- Customer Deposits.
- Gross Loans.
- Shareholders' Equity.

This enables investors to distinguish between businesses experiencing genuine operational growth and those merely maintaining historical performance.

---

## Formula

Horizontal Growth Rate

=

(Current Year − Previous Year)

×100

Financial statements provide historical snapshots.

Trend analysis transforms these snapshots into a moving picture of business performance.

It enables analysts to identify:

- Accelerating growth.

Rather than focusing solely on where a company is today, trend analysis evaluates the direction in which it is moving.

# Profitability Ratio Analysis

## Overview

Profitability represents the ultimate measure of business performance. While revenue growth, balance sheet expansion and market share provide useful indicators of organisational scale, profitability determines whether management is creating economic value for shareholders.

Commercial banks exist to transform customer deposits into profitable earning assets. Consequently, profitability analysis evaluates how effectively management converts lending activity, investment operations and fee-generating services into sustainable earnings.

Unlike the Historical Financial Statement Analysis, which focuses on absolute financial performance, profitability ratios standardise earnings relative to revenue, assets and shareholders' equity.

Within this model, profitability analysis evaluates four fundamental dimensions of financial performance:

- Net Profit Margin
- Net Interest Margin
- Return on Assets (ROA)
- Return on Equity (ROE)

Collectively, these ratios answer one critical investment question:

> **How efficiently does each institution convert its resources into shareholder wealth?**

---

# Net Profit Margin

## Purpose

Net Profit Margin measures the proportion of operating income ultimately retained as profit after deducting operating expenses, impairment charges, finance costs and taxation.

It is one of the clearest indicators of earnings quality because it measures management's ability to convert revenue into profits.

Higher profit margins generally indicate:

- Strong pricing power.
- Effective expense management.
- Lower credit losses.
- Higher operational efficiency.
- Better earnings quality.

---

## Formula

\[
\textbf{Net Profit Margin =
\frac{Net Profit}{Operating Income}
\times100}
\]

---

## Why It Matters

Revenue alone does not create shareholder value.

The critical question for investors is:

> **How much of every shilling earned ultimately belongs to shareholders?**

Two banks may generate identical operating income, yet the institution with stronger cost management, lower impairment charges and better operational discipline will retain a larger proportion as earnings.

Consequently, Net Profit Margin is widely regarded as one of the strongest indicators of business quality.

---

## Model Findings

| Institution | FY2025 |
|-------------|--------:|
| **KCB Group PLC** | **27.3%** |
| **NCBA Group PLC** | **32.6%** |

---

## Interpretation

NCBA converts approximately **33 cents** of every shilling of operating income into shareholder earnings.

KCB converts approximately **27 cents**.

Although KCB generated substantially larger absolute profits, NCBA demonstrated superior earnings conversion efficiency.

The higher margin indicates stronger cost discipline, lower earnings leakage and improved operational efficiency.

---

## What This Says About the Business

The results suggest that NCBA currently operates with a more efficient earnings model.

Its revenue is converted into profits more effectively than KCB despite operating at a smaller scale.

KCB continues to benefit from market leadership and larger operating capacity but sacrifices a greater proportion of revenue through operating costs and credit impairment.

---

## Investor Interpretation

Higher Net Profit Margins generally indicate:

- Higher quality earnings.
- Better operational execution.
- Greater resilience during economic downturns.
- Improved long-term shareholder value creation.

From a profitability perspective, the model suggests that NCBA currently demonstrates stronger earnings efficiency.

# Net Interest Margin (NIM)

## Purpose

Net Interest Margin measures the profitability generated from interest-earning assets after accounting for funding costs.

It evaluates how efficiently management transforms deposits and other funding sources into profitable lending activity.

For commercial banks, Net Interest Margin is one of the most closely monitored profitability indicators.

---

## Formula

\[
\textbf{Net Interest Margin =
\frac{Net Interest Income}{Average Interest-Earning Assets}}
\]

---

## Why It Matters

Banks generate profits by borrowing at relatively low interest rates and lending at higher rates.

The difference between these two rates represents the institution's economic spread.

Higher Net Interest Margins generally indicate:

- Better loan pricing.
- Lower funding costs.
- Strong deposit franchise.
- Effective treasury management.
- Higher earning asset productivity.

---

## Model Interpretation

The model demonstrates that both institutions continue generating healthy lending spreads.

However, NCBA's stronger growth in Net Interest Income suggests continued improvement in lending profitability and funding efficiency.

KCB benefits from a significantly larger earning asset base, enabling higher absolute interest income despite comparatively slower growth.

---

## Investor Interpretation

Consistently expanding Net Interest Margins generally indicate sustainable improvements in banking profitability.

Declining margins may signal:

- Higher funding costs.
- Competitive lending pressures.
- Weak loan pricing.
- Lower asset quality.

Consequently, Net Interest Margin serves as one of the strongest indicators of future earnings sustainability.

---

# Return on Assets (ROA)

## Purpose

Return on Assets measures how efficiently management utilises total assets to generate net earnings.

Since commercial banks possess exceptionally large balance sheets, ROA is often regarded as one of the most important indicators of operational efficiency.

---

## Formula

\[
\textbf{ROA=
\frac{Net Profit}{Average Total Assets}}
\]

---

## Why It Matters

Every asset held by a commercial bank represents deployed capital.

Management is expected to generate earnings from:

- Customer Loans.
- Treasury Investments.
- Government Securities.
- Interbank Placements.
- Investment Securities.

ROA evaluates whether these assets are being utilised efficiently.

---

## Model Findings

| Institution | FY2025 |
|-------------|--------:|
| **KCB Group PLC** | **2.73%** |
| **NCBA Group PLC** | **3.26%** |

---

## Interpretation

Although both institutions generated attractive returns relative to industry standards, NCBA demonstrated superior asset utilisation.

The higher ROA indicates that NCBA generated greater earnings for every shilling invested in productive assets.

---

## What This Says About the Business

The results suggest that NCBA currently extracts greater profitability from its balance sheet.

KCB maintains a substantially larger asset base but generates slightly lower returns relative to its size.

This distinction illustrates the difference between operational scale and operational efficiency.

---

## Investor Interpretation

Higher Return on Assets generally indicates:

- Superior management efficiency.
- Higher earnings quality.
- Better capital allocation.
- Stronger competitive positioning.

Long-term investors frequently favour institutions capable of generating higher returns without requiring disproportionately larger balance sheets.

---

# Return on Equity (ROE)

## Purpose

Return on Equity measures the rate of return earned on shareholders' invested capital.

Among all profitability ratios, ROE is arguably the most important because it directly measures shareholder wealth creation.

It answers one simple question:

> **How much profit does management generate for every shilling invested by shareholders?**

---

## Formula

\[
\textbf{ROE=
\frac{Net Profit}{Average Shareholders' Equity}}
\]

---

## Why It Matters

Shareholders invest capital with the expectation of earning attractive long-term returns.

ROE evaluates management's ability to allocate that capital efficiently.

Higher ROE generally indicates:

- Strong profitability.
- Efficient capital allocation.
- Sustainable earnings.
- Effective management execution.

---

## Model Findings

| Institution | FY2025 |
|-------------|--------:|
| **KCB Group PLC** | **17.54%** |
| **NCBA Group PLC** | **18.36%** |

---

## Interpretation

Both institutions generated attractive returns on shareholder capital.

However, NCBA produced a slightly higher ROE despite operating with a smaller balance sheet.

The difference appears modest numerically but becomes highly significant once decomposed using the DuPont Analysis later within the model.

---

## What This Says About the Business

At first glance, investors may conclude that both institutions create similar shareholder returns.

However, the DuPont Analysis demonstrates that these returns are generated through different mechanisms.

KCB relies more heavily upon financial leverage.

NCBA generates stronger profitability from its core operations.

This distinction has important implications for earnings quality and financial risk.

---

## Investor Interpretation

ROE should never be evaluated independently.

# DuPont Analysis

## Overview

The DuPont Analysis represents one of the most insightful components of the Comparative Bank Financial Model. While traditional profitability ratios such as Return on Equity (ROE) indicate the level of shareholder returns generated by an institution, they do not explain **how** those returns are achieved.

Two banks may report similar ROE figures while relying on fundamentally different business models. One institution may generate superior returns through strong operating profitability and efficient asset utilisation, whereas another may achieve comparable returns primarily through higher financial leverage.

The DuPont framework decomposes Return on Equity into its underlying drivers, enabling investors to distinguish between returns generated through operational excellence and returns amplified through leverage.

This analytical approach moves beyond measuring profitability to understanding the economic forces responsible for creating shareholder value.

---

# Purpose

The primary purpose of the DuPont Analysis is to explain **why** shareholder returns differ between institutions.

Specifically, the analysis seeks to determine:

- Which institution generates higher-quality earnings.
- Whether profitability or leverage drives shareholder returns.
- How efficiently assets are utilised.
- Whether management is allocating capital effectively.
- Whether current ROE levels are sustainable.
- Which institution creates superior long-term shareholder value.

Instead of asking:

> "Which bank has the higher Return on Equity?"

The DuPont Analysis asks:

> **"What factors are responsible for that Return on Equity?"**

---

# Why DuPont Analysis Matters

Return on Equity is one of the most widely used performance measures in banking because it evaluates how effectively management employs shareholders' capital.

However, ROE is an aggregate measure.

By itself, it provides no explanation regarding:

- Profitability.
- Operational efficiency.
- Asset utilisation.
- Financial leverage.

Consequently, investors cannot determine whether strong shareholder returns result from:

- Higher operating profits.
- Better asset management.
- Increased leverage.
- Superior capital allocation.

The DuPont framework resolves this limitation by decomposing ROE into measurable operating components.

For institutional investors, this provides a significantly deeper understanding of business quality than ROE alone.

---

# The Three-Step DuPont Model

The traditional DuPont framework decomposes Return on Equity into three distinct components:

\[
\boxed{
ROE =
Net\ Profit\ Margin
\times
Asset\ Turnover
\times
Equity\ Multiplier
}
\]

Each component evaluates a different aspect of management performance.

| Component | Formula | Measures |
|------------|---------|----------|
| Net Profit Margin | Net Profit ÷ Operating Income | Profitability |
| Asset Turnover | Operating Income ÷ Average Total Assets | Asset Utilisation |
| Equity Multiplier | Average Total Assets ÷ Average Equity | Financial Leverage |

Together, these three variables completely explain Return on Equity.

---

# Component One: Net Profit Margin

## Formula

Net Profit Margin

=

Net Profit

÷

Operating Income

---

## Purpose

Net Profit Margin measures how efficiently management converts operating income into profits attributable to shareholders.

It evaluates the institution's ability to control:

- Operating expenses.
- Credit impairment charges.
- Financing costs.
- Taxation.

Higher profit margins generally indicate:

- Better cost discipline.
- Higher earnings quality.
- Greater operational efficiency.
- Stronger pricing power.

---

| **KCB Group PLC** | **27.3%** |
| **NCBA Group PLC** | **32.6%** |

---

Although KCB generated significantly higher absolute profits, NCBA retains a larger proportion of revenue as earnings.

This indicates stronger operational efficiency and superior earnings conversion.

---

## What This Says About the Business

The higher margin demonstrates that NCBA currently operates a more efficient earnings model.

Management has successfully controlled operating costs and credit losses while maintaining strong revenue growth.

KCB continues to produce substantial profits through scale, but a larger proportion of revenue is absorbed by operating expenses and impairment charges.

---

## Investor Interpretation

Higher Net Profit Margins generally indicate:

- Better earnings quality.
- Stronger cost discipline.
- Greater resilience during economic downturns.
- Improved long-term shareholder value creation.

From an operational perspective, NCBA demonstrates stronger profitability.

---

# Component Two: Asset Turnover

## Formula

Asset Turnover

=

Operating Income

÷

Average Total Assets

---

## Purpose

Asset Turnover measures how efficiently management utilises total assets to generate operating income.

For commercial banks, productive assets include:

- Customer loans.
- Treasury investments.
- Government securities.
- Interbank placements.
- Investment securities.

The ratio evaluates whether these assets generate sufficient revenue relative to their size.

Instead, variations in shareholder returns originate elsewhere within the business model.

---

## Investor Interpretation

Comparable Asset Turnover suggests that both institutions have developed similarly efficient operating models.

Future improvements in shareholder returns are therefore more likely to arise from:

- Improved profitability.
- Better cost management.
- Enhanced capital allocation.

rather than greater asset productivity.

---

# Component Three: Equity Multiplier

## Formula

Equity Multiplier

=

Average Total Assets

÷

Average Shareholders' Equity

---

## Purpose

The Equity Multiplier measures financial leverage.

It indicates the extent to which management finances assets using shareholder capital versus external funding.

Higher leverage increases the potential return earned by shareholders but simultaneously increases financial risk.

## Investor Interpretation

Higher leverage is neither inherently positive nor negative.

During economic downturns, leverage magnifies financial losses.

Consequently, investors generally prefer institutions capable of generating attractive ROE without excessive reliance upon leverage.

# Comparative Valuation Analysis

## Overview

Financial statement analysis identifies institutions that demonstrate superior profitability, operational efficiency, earnings quality and financial resilience. However, even the strongest business may represent a poor investment if its market price already reflects those strengths.

Conversely, a fundamentally sound institution trading below its intrinsic value may present an attractive investment opportunity despite reporting comparatively lower financial performance.

The purpose of valuation is therefore not to determine which institution is the better business, but rather to determine which institution offers the better investment opportunity at its prevailing market price.

The Comparative Valuation Analysis integrates accounting information, market data and valuation techniques to estimate the economic value of each institution relative to its observed share price.

Unlike profitability ratios, which evaluate historical performance, valuation analysis is inherently forward-looking. It assesses how current market prices compare with the institution's ability to generate future earnings, dividends and shareholder value.

Within this model, valuation is performed using both market-based relative valuation multiples and intrinsic valuation techniques to produce a comprehensive assessment of investment attractiveness.

The analysis incorporates:

- Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio
- Price-to-Book (P/B) Ratio
- Enterprise Value to EBITDA (EV/EBITDA)
- Dividend Yield
- Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Valuation
- Intrinsic Value Estimation
- Margin of Safety Analysis

Collectively, these techniques provide multiple perspectives regarding whether each institution is fairly valued, undervalued or overvalued by the market.

---

# Purpose

The objective of the Comparative Valuation Analysis is to determine whether the market price of each institution accurately reflects its underlying financial performance and future earnings potential.

Specifically, the valuation analysis seeks to answer the following investment questions:

- Which institution trades at a lower valuation multiple?
- Which bank offers greater value relative to its earnings?
- Which institution provides stronger value relative to shareholders' equity?
- Is either bank trading below its intrinsic value?
- Which valuation methodology produces the strongest investment case?
- Which institution offers the larger margin of safety?
- Which investment provides superior long-term return potential?

Rather than selecting investments based solely on profitability or revenue growth, valuation analysis combines financial performance with market pricing to determine whether investors are paying an appropriate price for future cash flows.

Ultimately, valuation bridges the gap between financial analysis and investment decision-making.

---

# Why Valuation Matters

Exceptional businesses do not always produce exceptional investment returns.

If investors pay excessively high prices for high-quality businesses, future returns may be limited despite continued financial growth.

Similarly, fundamentally strong institutions may become attractive investment opportunities when temporary market pessimism causes their shares to trade below intrinsic value.

Valuation therefore introduces the concept of price discipline into investment analysis.

Professional investors rarely ask:

> "Which company is better?"

Instead, they ask:

> **"Which company offers the greatest value relative to its market price?"**

This distinction forms the foundation of value investing and institutional portfolio management.

---

# Relative Valuation

Relative valuation compares companies using standardised market multiples rather than estimating intrinsic value directly.

Rather than attempting to forecast future cash flows, relative valuation evaluates how the market currently prices each institution relative to its financial performance.

This approach is widely used by:

- Equity Research Analysts
- Investment Banks
- Portfolio Managers
- Asset Managers
- Institutional Investors

because it enables rapid comparison between peer institutions operating within similar industries.

Within this model, relative valuation incorporates:

- Price-to-Earnings Ratio
- Price-to-Book Ratio
- EV/EBITDA
- Dividend Yield

Each valuation multiple highlights a different aspect of market pricing.

Together, they provide a balanced assessment of relative investment attractiveness.

---

# Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio

## Purpose

The Price-to-Earnings Ratio measures how much investors are willing to pay today for one shilling of current earnings.

It is one of the most widely used valuation metrics because earnings ultimately determine long-term shareholder value.

---

## Formula

\[
\textbf{P/E Ratio =
\frac{Share Price}{Earnings Per Share}}
\]

---

## Why It Matters

A lower P/E ratio generally indicates that the market is paying less for each unit of earnings.

However, low valuation multiples are not automatically attractive.

Lower P/E ratios may also reflect:

- Weak growth expectations.
- Higher business risk.
- Declining profitability.
- Economic uncertainty.

Higher P/E ratios may indicate:

- Strong growth expectations.
- Superior earnings quality.
- Market leadership.
- Greater investor confidence.

Consequently, P/E should always be interpreted alongside profitability and growth.

---

## Model Interpretation

The model compares each institution's market valuation relative to its reported earnings.

Institutions generating stronger earnings while trading at lower earnings multiples may represent attractive value opportunities.

However, differences in growth expectations and risk profiles should also be considered before drawing investment conclusions.

---

# Price-to-Book (P/B) Ratio

## Purpose

The Price-to-Book Ratio compares market value with shareholders' equity.

Unlike industrial businesses, banks derive much of their value directly from financial assets and liabilities.

Consequently, Book Value represents a particularly important benchmark for banking valuation.

---

## Formula

\[
\textbf{P/B Ratio =
\frac{Share Price}{Book Value Per Share}}
\]

---

## Why It Matters

Banks trading below Book Value often attract attention from value investors.

A Price-to-Book Ratio below one may suggest:

- Market pessimism.
- Temporary earnings weakness.
- Undervaluation.
- Potential investment opportunity.

Conversely, institutions trading substantially above Book Value often reflect:

- Strong profitability.
- Superior franchise value.
- Higher expected returns.
- Strong investor confidence.

---

## Model Findings

The comparative analysis indicates differences in how the market values each institution relative to its accounting net worth.

These differences reflect varying expectations regarding future profitability, earnings growth and capital allocation efficiency.

Rather than interpreting Book Value independently, the model compares Price-to-Book alongside Return on Equity.

This relationship is particularly important because institutions generating higher ROE generally command higher Price-to-Book multiples.

---

## Investor Interpretation

One of the central questions addressed by this model is:

> **Does the institution's profitability justify its market valuation?**

Banks capable of consistently generating high ROE while trading at comparatively lower Price-to-Book multiples often present attractive value opportunities.

---

# Enterprise Value to EBITDA (EV/EBITDA)

## Purpose

EV/EBITDA compares the total value of the institution—including both debt and equity—to its operating earnings.

Although EV/EBITDA is more commonly applied to industrial companies, it can provide supplementary insight into relative valuation when comparing diversified financial institutions.

---

## Formula

\[
\textbf{EV/EBITDA =
\frac{Enterprise Value}{EBITDA}}
\]

---

## Why It Matters

Lower EV/EBITDA multiples generally indicate:

- Lower valuation.
- Greater acquisition attractiveness.
- Stronger value relative to operating earnings.

Higher multiples may indicate:

- Strong growth expectations.
- Premium market positioning.
- Higher expected profitability.

Because banking capital structures differ substantially from non-financial companies, EV/EBITDA should be interpreted alongside other banking-specific valuation metrics rather than independently.

---

# Dividend Yield

## Purpose

Dividend Yield measures the annual cash return received by shareholders relative to the current market price.

For income-focused investors, dividend yield represents an important component of total investment return.

---

## Formula

\[
\textbf{Dividend Yield =
\frac{Dividend Per Share}{Share Price}}
\times100
\]

---

## Why It Matters

Dividend Yield reflects management's willingness and ability to distribute earnings to shareholders.

Higher yields generally indicate:

- Strong cash generation.
- Stable profitability.
- Shareholder-friendly capital allocation.

However, unusually high dividend yields may also result from declining share prices.

Consequently, dividend analysis should always be supported by profitability and earnings sustainability.

---

# Intrinsic Value (Discounted Cash Flow)

## Overview

While relative valuation compares companies against each other, intrinsic valuation estimates what the business is fundamentally worth based on its expected future cash flows.

Rather than relying on current market sentiment, the Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) methodology estimates the present value of future economic benefits expected to be generated by the institution.

DCF is widely regarded as one of the most theoretically sound valuation techniques because it values businesses according to their capacity to generate future cash flows rather than prevailing market prices.

---

## Formula

\[
\textbf{Intrinsic Value =
\sum
\frac{FCF_t}{(1+r)^t}
+
\frac{TV}{(1+r)^n}}
\]

Where:

- FCF = Forecast Free Cash Flow
- r = Discount Rate
- TV = Terminal Value
- n = Forecast Period

---

## Purpose

The DCF model seeks to answer one fundamental investment question:

> **What is this institution actually worth?**

Unlike market multiples, intrinsic valuation is independent of investor sentiment.

It estimates value solely on the basis of future economic performance.

---

# Margin of Safety

The difference between intrinsic value and market price represents the Margin of Safety.

Benjamin Graham described the Margin of Safety as the cornerstone of intelligent investing because it provides protection against forecasting errors and unforeseen market events.

The larger the Margin of Safety, the greater the potential upside available to investors should market prices eventually converge toward intrinsic value.

---

# Comparative Valuation Summary

The valuation analysis demonstrates that financial performance alone is insufficient when selecting investments.

The strongest investment opportunities emerge when:

- High-quality businesses trade below intrinsic value.
- Strong profitability is available at reasonable valuation multiples.
- Market pessimism creates temporary mispricing.
- Intrinsic value materially exceeds current market price.

The model therefore integrates profitability analysis, DuPont decomposition and valuation techniques into a unified investment framework, enabling analysts to distinguish between operational excellence and investment attractiveness.

Ultimately, the Comparative Valuation Analysis transforms accounting performance into actionable investment decisions by answering the most important question in finance:

> **"Given current market prices, which institution offers the superior risk-adjusted investment opportunity?"**

# Sensitivity Analysis

## Overview

Financial valuation is inherently dependent upon assumptions regarding future business performance, macroeconomic conditions and investor expectations.

While the Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) model produces an estimate of intrinsic value based upon a defined set of assumptions, even small changes in those assumptions can materially affect the resulting valuation.

The Sensitivity Analysis evaluates the robustness of the valuation model by assessing how changes in key valuation drivers influence the estimated intrinsic value of each institution.

Rather than presenting valuation as a single definitive figure, the analysis recognises that valuation exists within a range of possible outcomes.

Consequently, sensitivity analysis provides investors with a more realistic understanding of potential upside, downside and investment risk.

---

# Purpose

The primary objective of the Sensitivity Analysis is to determine how sensitive the estimated intrinsic value is to changes in key modelling assumptions.

Specifically, the analysis seeks to evaluate:

- The impact of changes in the discount rate.
- The effect of different terminal growth assumptions.
- Changes in projected earnings growth.
- Variations in profitability assumptions.
- The robustness of the investment recommendation.
- The degree of valuation uncertainty.

Instead of asking:

> "What is the intrinsic value?"

Sensitivity Analysis asks:

> **"How much would intrinsic value change if our assumptions are wrong?"**

---

# Why Sensitivity Analysis Matters

Every financial model contains assumptions.

These assumptions include expectations regarding:

- Future revenue growth.
- Lending expansion.
- Net Interest Margins.
- Cost efficiency.
- Credit losses.
- Economic growth.
- Inflation.
- Interest rates.
- Cost of Equity.
- Terminal Growth Rate.

Since none of these variables are known with certainty, professional analysts evaluate multiple scenarios before making investment recommendations.

Sensitivity Analysis therefore transforms a static valuation into a dynamic decision-making framework.

---

# Methodology

The workbook evaluates multiple valuation scenarios by adjusting key assumptions while holding all other variables constant.

Typical scenarios include:

### Base Case

Represents management's expected financial performance using the primary modelling assumptions.

### Bull Case

Assumes stronger earnings growth, improving profitability and favourable macroeconomic conditions.

Generally results in:

- Higher intrinsic values.
- Larger upside potential.
- Greater shareholder returns.

### Bear Case

Assumes weaker earnings growth, declining profitability or deteriorating economic conditions.

Typically produces:

- Lower intrinsic values.
- Reduced margin of safety.
- Higher downside risk.

---

# Model Interpretation

The sensitivity analysis demonstrates that the valuation conclusions remain relatively stable across reasonable changes in modelling assumptions.

Although intrinsic values fluctuate as assumptions change, the comparative relationship between the two institutions remains broadly consistent.

This suggests that the investment conclusions derived from the valuation model are supported by the underlying financial performance rather than a single optimistic assumption.

---

# Investor Interpretation

Professional investors rarely rely upon a single valuation estimate.

Instead, they examine the range of possible intrinsic values under different economic scenarios.

A robust investment opportunity should continue to demonstrate attractive valuation characteristics across multiple reasonable assumptions.

The Sensitivity Analysis therefore increases confidence in the investment recommendation by demonstrating that the conclusions remain broadly consistent even when key assumptions are stressed.

Ultimately, this worksheet reinforces the credibility, transparency and reliability of the valuation process.
